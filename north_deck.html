Before (Fall 2012)

This picture was taken in fall 2012 after my dad and I rebuilt the bottom section of the deck (right side of the picture). We also did some work to replace the worst boards on the top section and level it out where it was being pushed up by the roots of the gigantic cottonwood tree. In 2016 we noticed the roots of the cottonwood tree were also pushing on the foundation of the house and that the decking on the top section had further deteriorated. Several of the posts on the perimeter of the deck had also rotted away. After 25+ years of service it was time to deconstruct it and replace it.


×

Deconstruction and Tree Removal (Summer 2016 - Spring 2017)

Mom acquired several quotes for the removal of the cottonwood tree and it was performed in the summer of 2016. Sadly, to access the cottonwood tree they also had to remove the seedless ash. Another annoyance was the roughly 2 feet of cottonwood stump sticking above the plane of the decking. That would result in me spending an entire day with various chainsaws reducing its height. My sister was incredibly helpful handling almost all the deconstruction of the old deck.

×

Construction Begins (Summer 2017)

The frame is a simple design made primarily out of green-treated 2x8 joists and 4x4 posts. On the perimeter of the deck there are 4x4 posts buried 42 inches deep every 48 inches (inside to inside distance). A single 2x8 around the inside and a pair of 2x6 split by a pair of 1x6 boards lining the base of the outside secures to each post. The 2x8 ledger boards lag bolted to the frame of the house and garage. There are 10 and 12 foot joists that meet in the middle where they lie atop a double 2x8 beam split by 4x4 posts buried 42 inches deep roughly every 48 inches. All joists are 16 inches center to center.

Finished Product

We worked ferverishly over the next 4 days to go from the partially completed frame shown above to the finished product. Most of the construction was very straightforward but the large volume of decking that had to be cut and secured to the frame meant some long days. We developed a system where I would cut decking and my sister would secure it. The lattice work on the railings took a fair bit of time but was not complicated. Thankfully the solid board exterior of the base of this deck was much easier than the lattice base on the south deck. The staircase coming off at 45° was a small challenge to get all the angles of the frame and railings correct.
