Post-Doctoral Research

At Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL) I work at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center (LANSCE) performing measurements on neutron capture and neutron-induced charged particle reactions. Prior to my fellowship my time was divided roughly equally between these activities. However, as a fellow my primary objective is the measurement of the 65Cu(n,γ) cross section.

An ideal instrument for measuring 65Cu(n,γ) is the Detector for Advanced Neutron Capture Experiments (DANCE), pictured below. DANCE is an array of 160 BaF2 in a close-packed geometry covering close to 4π. The large geometrical coverage and high segmentation allows DANCE to efficiently detect cascades of γ rays following neutron capture and yielding detailed information about the nature of the compound nucleus' decay.


DANCE is positioned on a 20.28-m flight path looking at a moderated spallation target. Using the known flight-path length and the measured Time-Of-Flight (TOF) we are able to determine the neutron energy event-by-event, allowing for simultaneous measurement across ~9 orders of magnitude of neutron energy ranging from meV to MeV.

Data on 65Cu(n,γ) has been collected and analysis is nearing completion so hopefully a link to a publication will follow in the near future.

Graduate Research

As a graduate student at Michigan State University I worked at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory (NSCL) in the β-decay spectroscopy group. The primary goal of my research was to understand the nuclear structure of the neutron-rich nuclei near the Z=28 proton shell and N=40 neutron sub-shell closures. In particular I studied the properties of 68,70Ni populated by the β decay of 68,70Co.

My thesis work was performed in two complimentary experiments carried out at NSCL in 2015. Starting with a 76Ge beam from the coupled-cyclotron facility at NSCL the ions of interest were created through projectile fragmentation, separated using the A1900 fragment separator, and transported to an experimental end station. This end station was comprised of a series of two Si PIN detectors in front of a planar Germanium Double-Sided Strip Detector (GeDSSD) surrounded by 16 detectors of the Segmented Germanium Array (SeGA) . All detectors were instrumented by XIA LLC DGF Pixie-16 digitizers . Shown below is a picture of a similar experiment run the year prior where SeGA is replaced by 9 germanium clover detectors


Ions incident on the PIN detectors are characterized by their energy loss through the first PIN detector and their time-of-flight between the A1900 fragment separator and that first PIN detector. Plotting the former vs the later causes ions of the same mass and charge to group together allowing for a cut on this two dimensional space to determine which isotope was implanted into the GeDSSD. The GeDSSD is a 1cm thick cylindrical crystal electrically segmented into 16 5-mm orthogonal strips on the front and back. Both the location (front strip and back strip) and time of implant are recorded. Some characteristic time later the implanted ion decays and we observe any coincident radiation with the decay.

One of the most interesting things we look for is the presence of internal transitions that occur predominately between 0+ states (since decay via γ-ray emission is forbidden). Using waveform (recorded detector signal as a function of time) processing techniques we identify the unique signature characteristic of detecting a β-decay electron followed in short succession by either internal conversion or internal pair production decay. This allows us to observe and study the nature of 0+ states. Combined with additional information from γ rays detected in coincidence with these internal transitions, experiments such as this allow us to investigate the underlying nuclear structure of exotic nuclei, the evolution of nuclear structure with changing nucleon number, and the quantum mechanics of configuration mixing and nuclear shape coexistence.

Undergraduate Research

At Minnesota State University, Mankato I worked on low-energy nuclear reaction measurements using proton and deuteron beams with bombarding energies up to 400 keV. The beams were produced by a AN400 Van de Graaff accelerator at the Minnesota State University, Mankato, Applied Nuclear Science Lab, pictured below.


The bulk of the work was centered around the refurbishment and characterization of the accelerator. Much of this was done measuring resonances in the 19F(p,α)16O* reaction. I was also part of the effort to develop a target system for producing and extracting 13N produced in the 12C(d,n)13N reaction, for use in physiological imaging via Positron Emission Tomography (PET).
